MS biomarkers OPR-021 separated biomarker roles: NfL predicted next-year relapse, while GFAP predicted PIRA. EPO-0364, EPO-0466, and EPO-0673 added nuance around timing, progression, and GFAP versus NfL. In otherwise stable MS with normal sNfL but repeatedly rising age-adjusted GFAP, what corroborating clinical, MRI, OCT, or cognitive findings would make you escalate surveillance or reconsider treatment, and would GFAP alone ever change your management?
  • JAMA Neurology GFAP/NfL progression study: GFAP and NfL appear to capture different inflammatory and progression biology, which supports risk-stratification discussion when GFAP rises without a parallel sNfL signal but does not establish GFAP-only treatment escalation.
  • Serum GFAP and PIRA in early relapsing MS: The study found sGFAP was associated with progression independent of relapse activity, but it does not define a therapy-change or surveillance threshold.
  • CMSC serum NfL best-practices guideline: Serum NfL monitoring is more operationalized than GFAP, leaving isolated GFAP elevation as a practical gap.
  • MAGNIMS-CMSC-NAIMS MRI consensus: MRI monitoring guidance supports surveillance decisions in MS, but it does not specify how a GFAP trend should trigger MRI, OCT, cognition testing, or treatment escalation.
Anti-CD20 MS risk EPO-0371 suggested durable ocrelizumab disease control, while EPO-0368, EPO-0137, OPR-118, and OPR-121 raised monitoring, discontinuation, and infection-risk questions. How do you approach older stable MS patients on anti-CD20 therapy with recurrent infections and falling IgG, including when to involve immunology, assess antibody function, alter dosing, consider IVIG, or discuss switch/de-escalation?
  • OCREVUS prescribing information: The label requires immunoglobulin monitoring and says to consider discontinuation with recurrent serious infections or prolonged hypogammaglobulinemia needing IVIG, but it gives no patient-level IgG threshold.
  • VELOCE vaccine-response study: Ocrelizumab attenuated humoral vaccine responses, supporting discussion of vaccine timing and clinically relevant antibody-function assessment rather than proving a routine testing algorithm for infected or hypogammaglobulinemic patients.
  • AAAAI secondary hypogammaglobulinemia work group report: The report supports assessing antibody function and considering immunology input, but it is not MS-specific.
  • Anti-CD20 immunoglobulin systematic review: Reported Ig trends and infection associations vary across studies, which supports uncertainty but does not itself provide management guidance for discontinuation or IVIG.
  • Neuroimmunology hypogammaglobulinemia management study: IVIG/SCIG, dose changes, cessation, and switching were all used, but the evidence is observational and does not define a single best strategy.
Natalizumab JCV/PML EPO-0996 found discordant PML risk classes depending on anti-JCV assay. EPO-0929 suggested host biology may also influence anti-JCV positivity. For a patient with JCV index 0.9-1.5 after more than 2 years of natalizumab and historically highly active MS, what factors make you choose q6-week extended-interval dosing versus switching, and how do MRI activity, prior immunosuppression, age, and risk tolerance shift that decision?
  • TYSABRI prescribing information: The label identifies anti-JCV positivity, treatment duration, and prior immunosuppression as PML risk factors, but it does not resolve extended-interval dosing versus switching.
  • Anti-JCV antibody index risk stratification: Index-based PML risk estimates make the 0.9-1.5 range clinically meaningful, but they are risk estimates rather than a management algorithm.
  • Natalizumab PML monitoring consensus: The consensus recommends more frequent MRI after 18 months in JCV-positive patients, but scan frequency and treatment continuation still require individualized judgment.
  • TOUCH registry extended-interval analysis: Observational data suggest lower PML risk with extended-interval dosing, but selection bias limits how confidently it can replace switching.
  • NOVA q6-week randomized trial: Six-week dosing provides randomized efficacy evidence, but the trial was not powered to settle PML-risk tradeoffs in higher-risk patients.
Seronegative vs false-positive neuroimmunology EPO-0664 supported antibody-negative autoimmune encephalitis as a real clinical entity, while EPO-0814 showed a high false-positive burden for serum-only CASPR2 results. EPO-0815 added mimic risk. In suspected neuroimmunologic disease with low-titer or serum-only antibody results, how do you weigh confirmatory testing, syndrome fit, MRI/CSF evidence, and exclusion of mimics before treating as antibody-negative AE or as a confirmed antibody-associated disorder?
  • Graus et al. autoimmune encephalitis criteria: The criteria allow antibody-negative AE when clinical, MRI, CSF, and exclusion findings support it, but they do not give a low-titer serum-antibody adjudication rule.
  • Autoimmune encephalitis misdiagnosis study: Overinterpretation of serum antibodies contributed to misdiagnosis, directly supporting the need to discuss confirmatory evidence before treatment.
  • Low-titer antibody overinterpretation case report: The case illustrates how low-titer antibodies can drive overtreatment when the clinical syndrome does not meet AE criteria.
  • International MOGAD diagnostic criteria: MOGAD diagnosis requires a compatible demyelinating event, assay context, and exclusion of alternatives, which makes borderline or low-positive results judgment-heavy rather than supporting a broad seronegative MOGAD label.
  • NEMOS NMOSD diagnostic recommendations: Updated NMOSD guidance emphasizes differential diagnosis and AQP4/MOG distinction, but real-time weighting of discordant antibody, MRI, and CSF data remains unresolved.
gMG goals OPR-085 showed patient-acceptable symptom state is not captured fully by low MG-ADL. EPO-0152, EPO-0146, and EPO-0308 separated symptom response, sustained outcomes, steroid burden, and early exacerbation risk. For patients with MG-ADL 0-2 whose patient-acceptable symptom state, steroid burden, rescue/exacerbation history, or adverse-effect burden remains discordant, what finding most often makes you decide that current control is not good enough?
  • International consensus guidance for MG management: Expert guidance supports treatment goals but does not define how to weigh MSE, PASS, steroid dose, rescue history, and adverse effects in a low MG-ADL patient.
  • MGFA post-intervention status standards: Minimal manifestations and remission categories standardize outcomes, but they do not specify when low residual symptoms are acceptable under high treatment burden.
  • Patient-acceptable symptom state study: PASS thresholds include MG-ADL scores around 2, which can diverge from stricter minimal symptom expression definitions.
  • MG-ADL utilization review: The review shows that MG-ADL supports multiple constructs, including response, PASS, and MSE, which can produce different impressions of control.
  • MM-or-better severity cutoffs study: Proposed quantitative cutoffs underscore that clinician-assigned MM status, patient acceptability, and steroid-sparing goals do not map perfectly.
CIDP/immune neuropathy EPO-0052 highlighted POEMS misdiagnosed as CIDP. EPO-0053 described acute-onset CIDP. EPO-0211 and EPO-0219 pointed to nodopathy and poor IVIG response. In possible CIDP with atypical features, weak objective IVIG response, or negative nodal/paranodal antibody testing, how do you structure reassessment before continuing CIDP-directed treatment, pursuing repeat antibody or mimic testing, or relabeling as a CIDP variant or alternative diagnosis?
  • EAN/PNS CIDP guideline: The guideline separates CIDP variants from autoimmune nodopathies and supports antibody testing in selected cases, but it does not provide a simple stop-or-relabel rule for weak IVIG response or mimic reassessment.
  • CIDP misdiagnosis review: Atypical phenotypes, equivocal electrodiagnostics, CSF overreliance, and subjective treatment benefit are recurring sources of misdiagnosis.
  • CIDP diagnostic pitfalls study: Perceived benefit can reinforce a CIDP label even when objective evidence is weak, supporting structured reassessment.
  • CIDP mimics case series: Alternative diagnoses can satisfy parts of the CIDP workup, including electrodiagnostic criteria, which makes mimic review necessary before long-term treatment.
  • IVIG withdrawal trial in stable CIDP: Withdrawal can be feasible in stable patients, but it does not resolve atypical possible-CIDP cases with weak objective response.
EVT boundaries OPR-127 supported IVT+EVT in isolated M2 occlusion, while EPO-0677 found similar outcomes but higher sICH for broader distal-to-medium vessel occlusions. EPO-0521 raised ultra-late EVT uncertainty. After negative medium/distal-vessel EVT trials and current guideline recommendations, in which rare MeVO cases, if any, would you still transfer or treat, and how do disabling deficit, dominant-branch anatomy, salvageable tissue, collaterals, and access risk affect that decision?
  • AHA/ASA 2026 acute ischemic stroke guideline: Updated recommendations define broad reperfusion pathways, but rare medium-vessel edge cases still depend on anatomy, time window, imaging, and local systems.
  • ESO-ESMINT thrombectomy guideline: M2 EVT support is partly consensus-based, and evidence for more distal or medium-vessel occlusions is lower certainty.
  • DISTAL randomized trial: EVT did not improve disability or death in distal/medium-vessel occlusion overall, challenging routine expansion beyond stronger guideline populations.
  • ESCAPE-MeVO randomized trial: No 90-day functional benefit was seen for medium-vessel EVT, leaving the question of severe-deficit or favorable-imaging exceptions unresolved.
  • JAMA Network Open DMVO cohort: Older observational data provide lower-level, hypothesis-generating context for selected distal-occlusion exceptions, but functional independence was not clearly improved.
Tenecteplase late/remote stroke EPO-0188 showed workflow gains in a remote region. EPO-0193 suggested late-window tenecteplase benefit in selected settings. EPO-0517 framed the efficacy-safety tradeoff versus alteplase. For perfusion-selected 4.5-24h LVO patients at a non-EVT hospital, when, if ever, would you give tenecteplase before transfer, given neutral late-window transfer-relevant data, TRACE-III's no-EVT-access population, hemorrhage risk, imaging confidence, BP control, and rescue protocols?
  • AHA/ASA 2026 acute ischemic stroke guideline: Tenecteplase has a larger role in current acute-stroke guidance, but remote-hospital late-window workflows still require local protocol decisions.
  • TNKase prescribing information: The label supports AIS dosing and monitoring but specifies an early treatment window, which leaves 4.5-24h use dependent on trial and guideline interpretation.
  • TIMELESS trial: Perfusion-selected ICA/M1/M2 patients treated 4.5-24h had neutral disability outcomes overall, making patient selection and transfer timing central.
  • TRACE-III trial: In patients without EVT access, late-window tenecteplase increased 90-day mRS 0-1 but also increased symptomatic intracranial hemorrhage, so it is relevant but not direct evidence for pre-transfer use.
  • ETERNAL-LVO trial: Early termination and neutral overall results leave any EVT-transfer subgroup signal hypothesis-generating rather than protocol-defining.
Breakthrough stroke on DOAC OPR-102 and OPR-123 emphasized competing mechanisms and correctable contributors after breakthrough AF stroke. OPR-124 found continuation versus switching did not clearly separate outcomes. In AF patients with ischemic stroke despite apparently therapeutic DOAC, how do you first separate adherence, dosing, renal function, interactions, and competing mechanisms from true anticoagulant failure, and when no correctable cause is found, how do you weigh continuing the same DOAC, switching, or LAAO evaluation?
  • AHA/ASA 2021 secondary stroke prevention guideline: The guideline provides the secondary-prevention framework, but a therapeutic DOAC-breakthrough algorithm is not specified.
  • EHRA 2021 NOAC practical guide: The guide emphasizes adherence, dosing, renal function, interactions, and competing mechanisms after stroke on a NOAC, but it does not provide randomized switching rules or make antiplatelet escalation a default AF-breakthrough strategy.
  • Seiffge et al. pooled cohort analysis: Changing anticoagulant type was not clearly associated with lower recurrence, which weakens empiric switching as a default answer.
  • JAMA Network Open 2026 DOAC strategy study: Switching after breakthrough stroke was not associated with a clinically meaningful short-term advantage over continuing the same DOAC.
  • JAMA Neurology 2024 LAAO cohort: LAAO was associated with fewer recurrent ischemic strokes, but randomized confirmation is still needed.
  • 2023 ACC/AHA/ACCP/HRS AF guideline summary: LAAO is mainly framed around OAC contraindication or high bleeding risk, leaving therapeutic DOAC-breakthrough stroke without bleeding contraindication as a gray zone.
Anti-amyloid AD treatment OPR-076 showed real-world amyloid lowering with lecanemab/donanemab. EPO-0166 raised regional residual amyloid dynamics. EPO-0007 raised ARIA monitoring uncertainty. After apparent global amyloid clearance on anti-amyloid therapy, how do you interpret focal cortical retention across visual, Centiloid, and regional PET findings, and how do tau, fluid biomarkers, MRI, and clinical course shape stop, maintain, repeat-PET, or restart discussions?
  • AA/SNMMI amyloid and tau PET appropriate-use criteria: Amyloid PET is appropriate for treatment eligibility and response monitoring, but tau PET monitoring after treatment is rated uncertain.
  • KISUNLA prescribing information: The label allows stopping based on amyloid PET reduction to minimal levels, but additional dosing after the pivotal-trial period is not well defined.
  • LEQEMBI prescribing information: Lecanemab uses continued or maintenance dosing after 18 months rather than the same PET-clearance stopping rule, creating a product-level implementation difference.
  • TRAILBLAZER-ALZ 2: Amyloid-PET response guided donanemab discontinuation in the trial, but the trial does not specify how to act on focal cortical retention after global clearance.
  • SNMMI/EANM amyloid PET procedure standard: Visual interpretation with quantitative and MRI context is supported, but the standard predates routine treatment-stop or restart decisions.
Biological AD diagnosis EPO-0499 showed reclassification under 2024 biological criteria, including a non-AD/prion concern. EPO-0493 focused on p-tau217 gray-zone classification. EPO-0326 showed biomarker thresholds vary by pathology burden. In atypical or mixed-pathology presentations with gray-zone p-tau217, how do you decide whether to pursue amyloid PET, CSF ratios, tau PET, or longitudinal follow-up before treating AD as the actionable diagnosis?
  • Alzheimer's Association 2024 revised criteria: The criteria define AD biologically and distinguish core biomarkers from staging markers, but they do not provide a practical sequence for atypical gray-zone p-tau217 cases.
  • AA/SNMMI amyloid and tau PET appropriate-use criteria: Amyloid and tau PET are appropriate in atypical or mixed presentations, but sequencing against CSF and blood biomarkers remains individualized.
  • Alzheimer's Association blood-based biomarker guideline: Performance-based, brand-agnostic recommendations caution that blood tests must be interpreted within the diagnostic context.
  • Plasma p-tau217 three-range study: Low, intermediate, and high reference ranges support triage, but intermediate results require confirmatory testing rather than immediate diagnostic closure.
  • Two-step p-tau217 workflow study: Confirmatory CSF testing for uncertain blood-test strata supports the question of how to sequence tests before treating AD as actionable.
Migraine response EPO-0088 showed MIDAS/HIT-6 are not interchangeable with headache diaries. EPO-0409 and EPO-0410 showed non-pain and prodromal symptoms can persist despite headache improvement. When monthly migraine days, acute-medication use, disability scores, PGIC, and patient-selected non-headache symptoms move in different directions, what pattern of benefit is enough for you to continue, switch, or combine preventive therapy?
  • NICE TA871 eptinezumab recommendations: Stopping thresholds are based on migraine-frequency reduction, leaving discordant disability, PGIC, acute-medication, and non-headache symptom domains as implementation modifiers.
  • European Headache Federation CGRP-mAb guideline: Response assessment remains centered on headache or migraine days, so patient-centered below-threshold benefit is not fully specified.
  • CGRP antibody treatment-success study: Responder classification differed when monthly migraine days, MIDAS, and HIT-6 were used, directly supporting discussion of discordant endpoints.
  • PGIC versus monthly migraine-day persistence study: Patient global impression may diverge from frequency-based response and may influence continuation decisions.
  • Patient-identified most bothersome symptom study: Patient-selected non-headache symptoms can be trial endpoints, but they are not clearly integrated into payer or guideline stopping rules.
